How does an ESG rating work?

A rating isn't one number pulled from thin air. It's a stack: a global score built from weighted dimensions, themselves built from dozens of criteria that change with the industry you're in.

The anatomy of a score

Four levels, from the headline number down to the data

Reading top-to-bottom: the score you see at the top is assembled from the layers beneath it. Each layer is weighted, and the weights are decided by what actually matters for the company's industry.

01

Global S&P Global ESG Score0–100

The headline figure — a sum of the weighted dimension scores. It's a relative score: where the company sits versus peers in the same industry, not an absolute pass/fail.

02

Three dimension scoresE · S · G&Econ

Environmental, Social, and Governance & Economic. Each is a weighted roll-up of its criteria, then adjusted downward where corporate ESG controversies apply.

03

30+ criteriaindustry-specific

The building blocks. The set of criteria — and how heavily each one is weighted — differs by industry. Here we're looking at food & beverage. The weight of a criterion depends on its materiality for that sector.

04

Industry classificationGICS

Everything above hinges on which industry a company is placed in. S&P Global uses the Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) to set a company's CSA industry — which in turn determines its criteria and weights.

GICS

Jointly developed by MSCI and S&P Global Market Intelligence, GICS is the classification both major rating houses lean on to decide what a company should be compared against. Get the industry wrong and the whole stack of weights is wrong.

Why industry matters

Every industry has different pressure points

What's relevant for one sector isn't necessarily relevant for another. The risk and importance of biodiversity loss in food & beverage is far more elevated than in banking — so it carries far more weight. That judgement is made through materiality, and S&P Global takes a double-materiality view.

Outside → in

Financial materiality

How social and environmental factors affect the company's value. Climate regulation, water scarcity or supply shocks land on the balance sheet — these are risks and opportunities to the business itself.

Inside → out

Impact materiality

How the company affects society and the environment. Its footprint on ecosystems, communities and workers — the company's impact on the world around it, regardless of whether it shows up financially.

Who produces the ratings

The main rating houses

S&P Global is one of three names that dominate the space. The others run their own methodologies, scales and definitions of what's material — which is exactly why the same company can land very different scores depending on who you ask.

The one above ↑

S&P Global ESG Scores

0–100 · percentile vs peers

Peer-relative score built on the Corporate Sustainability Assessment (CSA). Integrates with S&P's traditional credit-analysis infrastructure. Feeds the Sustainability Yearbook and the Dow Jones Best-in-Class indices.

Big three

MSCI ESG Ratings

AAA → CCC · letter

Industry-relative, rules-based methodology that ranks leaders and laggards within a GICS sub-industry. Established 1999; widely used for index construction. In 2024 MSCI struck a data partnership with Moody's.

Big three

Morningstar Sustainalytics

Risk · negligible → severe

Measures unmanaged ESG risk to enterprise value — lower number means lower risk, the inverse of S&P's logic. Broadest coverage (15,000+ companies, incl. private). Separates its controversy rating from the core score.

ISS ESG

Performance scale A+ to D−, benchmarked against sector peers; "Prime" status for leaders.

CDP

Disclosure-led grades A to D−, scored from an annual climate, water & forests questionnaire.

LSEG (ex-Refinitiv / Asset4)

Transparent public-data scores across 15,000+ companies.

Sustainable Fitch

ESG ratings for companies and individual financial instruments, from the Fitch group.

Bloomberg

Real-time, market-responsive ESG data built off its financial-data backbone.

EcoVadis

The go-to for supply-chain and supplier-level sustainability assessment.

So why do we want ratings?

What a rating is actually for

01

Comparability

A common yardstick to line companies up against their peers — the whole point of an industry-relative score.

02

Risk & opportunity

A read on sustainability-related risks before they hit the bottom line, and on where the upside sits.

03

Screening & index construction

Ratings feed ETFs, best-in-class indices and exclusion screens — they drive where capital flows.

04

Regulation & reporting

The EU's ESG Rating Regulation (2024/3005) starts applying to providers in July 2026, pushing methodology transparency and conflict-of-interest rules.

The catch

Ratings disagree — and some firms build their own

Because each house defines materiality, weights and scope differently, the same company can score high with one provider and middling with another. Researchers call it "aggregate confusion"; scope divergence — which issues even get measured — is the biggest driver.

That's partly why a lot of the production happens in-house: large asset managers run proprietary ESG models rather than relying on a single vendor — to control the methodology, fill coverage gaps, and avoid inheriting someone else's disagreement. Buying two providers and blending them is another common fix.
